Digital Law and Policy Centre Pvt. Ltd. is a Kathmandu-based legal services and research organisation incorporated under the laws of Nepal. We sit at the intersection of traditional legal practice and emerging digital governance — bringing rigour to questions of law, rights, and public policy.

We work with individuals, civil society organisations, government bodies, and international institutions to advance legal literacy, provide expert counsel, and conduct research that shapes policy across Nepal and the broader South Asian region.

Our work spans court representation, legal documentation, investor facilitation, public interest advocacy, and the growing field of digital rights — grounded in Nepal's domestic legal framework and international human rights standards.

Digital Laws in Force — Know Your Rights

Each card summarises a law currently in force. Click Know Your Rights to flip the card and see a plain-language list of what that law means for you as a citizen or user in Nepal.

Source: Nepal Law Commission · MoCIT · Updated May 2025

Digital / Cyber · In Force
● In Force
Electronic Transactions Act, 2063
Enacted 2008 · ETA · Nepal Law Commission
Nepal's foundational digital law. Governs electronic records, digital signatures, and cybercrime including hacking, phishing, and unauthorised access to computer systems. Forms the legal basis for most digital rights enforcement in Nepal.
Key Limitation
Drafted for early internet — does not cover AI, data protection, platform liability, or algorithmic harms.
Your Rights Under the ETA
Electronic Transactions Act, 2063 (2008)
  • Your digital signature on electronic documents is legally valid and equivalent to a handwritten signature
  • Electronic contracts, online agreements, and digital documents are legally binding and enforceable in Nepali courts
  • You have the right to file a police complaint if someone hacks your accounts, accesses your devices without permission, or phishes your data
  • Publishing or distributing someone else's private digital information or images without consent is a criminal offence under this Act
  • Cyberstalking and targeted online harassment are covered offences — you can report to the Cyber Bureau, Nepal Police
  • You are protected if someone impersonates you online or creates fake profiles in your name
Privacy · In Force
● In Force
Individual Privacy Act, 2075
Enacted 2018 · वैयक्तिक गोपनियता सम्बन्धी ऐन
Nepal's primary privacy law. Establishes that every person has the right to privacy of their body, residence, documents, data, and communications. Requires consent before personal data can be collected or used — including electronic and digital data.
Key Limitation
No independent Data Protection Authority. No data breach notification requirement. No provisions on cookies or cross-border transfers.
Your Rights Under the Privacy Act
Individual Privacy Act, 2075 (2018)
  • No person or institution can collect, record, or use your personal data without your explicit consent
  • You have the right to access any personal information held about you by any person or organisation
  • You can demand correction or deletion of inaccurate personal information held about you
  • Your electronic data — messages, emails, browsing, location — cannot be accessed or monitored without your consent or a legal order
  • Sharing your private images, videos, or personal information without your consent is a punishable offence
  • Any data collected about you must be used only for the specific purpose you consented to — it cannot be sold or repurposed
  • You can file a complaint at your local district court if your privacy rights are violated
Platform Regulation · In Force
● In Force
Social Media Directive (DMSM, 2080)
Enacted November 2023 · MoCIT Directive
Requires all social media platforms operating in Nepal to register with the Ministry of Communication and Information Technology (MoCIT), appoint local grievance officers, and moderate content in Nepali. Enforced via the September 2025 blocking of 26 platforms including Facebook and Instagram.
Concern
No judicial oversight for content removal orders. Vague prohibited content categories could suppress legitimate speech.
Your Rights Under the Social Media Directive
Directive on Regulating Social Media Use, 2080 (2023)
  • Social media platforms operating in Nepal must be registered and must have a local grievance officer you can contact
  • You have the right to submit complaints to the platform's local representative about content or account-related issues
  • Platforms must respond to your complaints within the timeframes specified by the Directive
  • Children are specifically protected — platforms must take action against content involving sexual exploitation or abuse of minors
  • Cyberbullying, spreading false information, and impersonation are prohibited actions on social media under this Directive
  • Your right to privacy must be respected by platforms — hacking, phishing, and identity theft facilitated through social media are prohibited
Access to Information · In Force
● In Force
Right to Information Act, 2064
Enacted 2007 · सूचनाको हक सम्बन्धी ऐन
Guarantees every citizen the right to request and receive information held by public bodies. Establishes the National Information Commission to hear appeals when information is denied. One of Nepal's most important transparency laws.
Note
Applies to government bodies and public institutions — private companies are not generally covered unless performing public functions.
Your Rights Under RTI
Right to Information Act, 2064 (2007)
  • You have the right to request any information held by government ministries, departments, local bodies, and public institutions
  • Public bodies must respond to your RTI request within 15 days (or 7 days for urgent matters)
  • If your request is denied, you can appeal first to the head of the institution and then to the National Information Commission
  • You do not need to give a reason for requesting information — just submit a written application to the Information Officer of the institution
  • Officials who wrongfully withhold information can be fined and disciplined under this Act
  • You can use RTI to obtain government contracts, spending records, policy documents, and official decisions affecting the public
Intellectual Property · In Force
● In Force
Copyright Act, 2059
Enacted 2002 · प्रतिलिपि अधिकार ऐन
Protects the intellectual property rights of creators of literary, artistic, musical, and digital works. Relevant to online content creators, bloggers, musicians, and anyone whose work is used or reproduced online without permission.
Note
Nepal's Copyright Act predates the social media era and does not specifically address platform liability or AI-generated content.
Your Rights Under Copyright Law
Copyright Act, 2059 (2002)
  • Any original work you create — writing, art, music, code, video — is automatically protected by copyright the moment it is created
  • No one can reproduce, distribute, or publish your work online or offline without your permission
  • You have the moral right to be credited as the author — even if you assign copyright to someone else
  • If someone uses your content on social media, YouTube, or websites without permission, you can demand removal and claim compensation
  • Copyright in Nepal lasts for your lifetime plus 50 years after your death
  • You can report copyright violations to the Department of Industry and file a complaint with the court for damages
Criminal / Digital · In Force
● In Force
National Penal Code, 2074 — Digital Provisions
Enacted 2017 · मुलुकी अपराध संहिता
The National Penal Code contains specific provisions relevant to the digital space — including criminalising online defamation, non-consensual sharing of intimate images, cyberbullying, and fraud committed through digital means.
Note
Overlaps with the Electronic Transactions Act on several digital offences. Can be used alongside ETA for stronger legal remedies.
Digital Rights in the Penal Code
National Penal Code, 2074 (2017)
  • Sharing intimate or private images without consent (often called "revenge porn") is a criminal offence — you can file an FIR at your local police station
  • Online fraud, digital cheating, and scams are criminal offences — you can report to the Cyber Bureau or local police
  • Defamation published online carries criminal liability — but truth is a valid defence
  • Threatening someone through digital messages, calls, or social media is a criminal offence
  • You have the right to file a private complaint (firaad) in addition to a police report for crimes committed against you online
  • Sexual harassment committed online or through digital means is covered under the Sexual Harassment at Workplace Act and the Penal Code

Bills currently under parliamentary consideration or ministerial review. These are not yet law but will significantly shape Nepal's digital future.

Platform Regulation · 2025
◐ Pending Parliament
Social Media Act (Bill), 2081
Registered at National Assembly · January 28, 2025
Proposes mandatory licensing for all social media platforms, government authority to refuse or revoke licences on national security grounds, penalties up to NPR 3 lakh or 2 years imprisonment for hate speech and impersonation, and prohibition of anonymous accounts.
Key Concern
No safe harbour provision. Criminalises anonymous speech. Vague content categories risk suppressing legitimate expression. UNESCO flagged compatibility issues with international free speech standards.
What This Bill Means For You
Social Media Act (Bill), 2081 — Proposed, not yet law
  • ⚠ Anonymous accounts would be prohibited — platforms must verify user identity
  • ⚠ Liking, sharing, or commenting on prohibited content could attract criminal liability under the proposed law
  • ⚠ "National security," "social harmony," and "hate speech" are not clearly defined — creating risk of broad interpretation against dissent
  • ✓ The Bill does propose a formal grievance mechanism for users against platforms
  • ✓ Platforms would be required to respond to complaints within specified timeframes
  • ⚠ Civil society, the Rastriya Swatantra Party, and international bodies have called for the Bill's withdrawal or significant revision
Cybersecurity · 2024
◐ Under Review
IT and Cybersecurity Bill, 2080
Introduced 2024 · Under Ministerial Review
Aims to replace the Electronic Transactions Act. Covers critical infrastructure protection, mandatory annual security audits for critical sectors, and delivery of public services through electronic means. Contains progressive provisions alongside concerns about privacy and surveillance.
Key Concern
Clauses permitting government access to private communications without sufficient judicial oversight. Overbroad online behaviour restrictions that could conflict with ICCPR Art 17 and 19.
What This Bill Means For You
IT and Cybersecurity Bill, 2080 — Draft, not yet law
  • ✓ Banks, hospitals, and government systems would be required to pass annual cybersecurity audits — stronger protection for your data
  • ✓ Government services would be available electronically — reducing need to physically attend government offices
  • ⚠ The Bill contains clauses allowing authorities to access private communications — potentially without adequate judicial oversight
  • ⚠ Vague restrictions on online behaviour could be used to prosecute ordinary citizens for legitimate online activity
  • ⚠ Duplicates social media licensing requirements already in the Social Media Bill — creating legal confusion about which law applies

Identified Policy Gaps

Critical areas where Nepal has no law, or where existing law is inadequate. These gaps leave citizens and businesses legally unprotected.

Data Protection · 2025
✗ No Legislation
No Comprehensive Data Protection Law
Gap Identified · Privacy Act 2018 Insufficient
Nepal has no dedicated data protection authority and no comprehensive data protection law. The Privacy Act 2018 provides partial coverage but lacks a supervisory authority, data breach notification requirements, data portability rights, and cross-border transfer rules.
Why It Matters
India's DPDP Act 2023 has cross-border implications affecting Nepali data processors. Nepal risks being excluded from data-sharing agreements with countries requiring adequate protection standards.
Rights You Don't Currently Have
Comprehensive Data Protection — Gap Analysis
  • No independent Data Protection Authority to complain to if companies misuse your data
  • No legal right to be notified if a company suffers a data breach and your information is exposed
  • No legal right to data portability — to receive your data from one platform and move it to another
  • No rules governing how your data can be transferred to companies in other countries
  • No sector-specific data protection rules for healthcare, finance, or education
  • Recommended: Nepal should amend the Privacy Act 2018 or enact a standalone data protection law with an independent supervisory authority
Cybercrime · International
✗ Not Acceded
Budapest Convention on Cybercrime
Council of Europe · 2001 · Nepal Not a Party
The only binding international cybercrime treaty, enabling cross-border cooperation in evidence gathering and prosecution. Nepal has not acceded. As ransomware, online fraud, and sextortion targeting Nepali victims increasingly originate abroad, the absence of a formal cross-border framework creates serious enforcement gaps.
Recommended Action
Nepal should formally assess accession to enable mutual legal assistance in cross-border cybercrime cases, particularly affecting migrant workers and students abroad.
Why This Gap Affects You
Budapest Convention on Cybercrime — Gap Analysis
  • If you are defrauded online by someone in another country, Nepali police have very limited ability to pursue the case across borders
  • Nepali migrant workers are disproportionately targeted by cross-border online scams — without this treaty, perpetrators in other countries face little accountability
  • Evidence stored on servers in other countries (WhatsApp data, email records) cannot be easily obtained by Nepali authorities for domestic prosecutions
  • Sextortion and image-based abuse originating from abroad is extremely difficult to prosecute without international cooperation frameworks
  • Nepal's IT & Cybersecurity Bill 2024 acknowledges this gap but does not resolve it — accession to the Budapest Convention would be the direct solution
